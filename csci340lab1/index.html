A Brief History of the Kentucky Cave Wars

In the early 20th century in Kentucky the difficulty of farming the poor soil, combined with the massive tourist success of Mammoth Cave, sparked a boom in the excavation and exploration of caves. This was called the Kentucky Cave Wars. During this period, many farmers opened small tourist businesses giving tours of caves that they had found on their property. Many resorted to underhanded buisiness tactics, such as decieving tourists into thinking that Mammoth Cave was closed, to drive business to their caves. A number of incidents and the decline of this tourism boom resulted in the establishment of the Mammoth Cave National Park.

Notable Caves

  • Mammoth Cave
  • Great Onyx Cave
  • Floyd Collins' Crystal Cave
  • Sand Cave

A notable incident that heralded the end of the Kentucky Cave Wars was the 1925 incident in Sand Cave. Floyd Collins, a member of the family that owned Great Crystal Cave (now named Floyd Collins' Crystal Cave) was exploring a nearby cave called Sand Cave in an effort to expand his family's buisiness when he was trapped in a small cave in. Local efforts began to rescue him, and immediatly hit roadblocks due to how narrow and deep the cave was. His plight gained international media attention, and despite engineers and excavator traveling from all over the country to rescue him, he died of exposure after being trapped for 14 days. He was reached by a rescue crew three days later. His death helped spark the movement that resulted in the founding of the Mammoth Cave National Park and the end of the Kentucky Cave Wars
